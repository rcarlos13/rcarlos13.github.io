UpDish

Improving the restaurant ordering experience of the socially anxious

Overview

UpDish was my capstone project at Centennial College’s Interactive Media Management postgraduate program. I led two classmates as the Project Manager and Product Designer, taking the project from concept to prototype using a design thinking methodology.

Problem Statement

People with social anxiety go through an overwhelming experience when ordering in person at build-your-own-meal restaurants because they don’t have a comfortable way to learn how to order properly at such places.

Proposed Solution

UpDish is a learning website that helps users understand the menu and ordering process at build-your-own-meal restaurants so they can then confidently place their orders in person.

Duration

January to April 2020 (4 months)

Empathize

Finding a Starting Point

Customization is a growing trend in the food industry, leading to more restaurants where you can modify your meal’s ingredients or even build it from scratch. I came up with the project’s concept after noticing how ordering at such restaurants is baffling for many people. I reckoned it’s even harder for socially anxious individuals, so I wanted to build a platform that tackles this problem.

I looked at popular build-your-own-meal restaurants to see roughly how many customizations are typically available. Having numerous options can be good since there’ll always be something different to buy each time. However, too many of it can paralyze a customer’s decision-making and cause them to avoid ordering altogether.

Conducting User Interviews

Wanting to validate that the food customization trend has its disadvantages, I interviewed around ten individuals about their personal experiences when ordering at build-your-own-meal restaurants.

Key interview insights

  • First-time customers find that employees generally expect everyone to know how the restaurant works, and so, they never receive proper guidance when ordering.
  • When learning how to order at a restaurant, some will only ask employees who look friendly and able to communicate clearly. Others will make someone else order for them instead.
  • Regulars at particular restaurants are often interested in everything those places offer; still, because the seemingly complex menu confounds them, they resort to familiarity and end up buying the same thing.
  • A few individuals admitted they mentally rehearse what they’re going to say when ordering verbatim and panic if asked an unanticipated question.

My biggest takeaway was discovering that introversion is not synonymous with social anxiety. The target audience I originally had in mind for my platform was introverted people. I always assumed they avoid socializing because it makes them anxious, and by that logic, they automatically have social anxiety.

In actuality, introversion is when being alone keeps you refreshed and energized. So you can be an introvert without social anxiety because you don’t feel apprehensive when socializing—unlike those who are socially anxious.

Researching the Users

I researched social anxiety to understand this mental health condition better and narrow down my users further. I found that it’s most prevalent in adults aged 18 to 29, while the sex ratio is about the same.

Although everyone gets anxious, people with social anxiety disorder or social phobia have this intense fear of “being humiliated or scrutinized in social situations;” their anxiety is persistent and severe to the point where it causes significant impairment in their daily lives. Something as nonchalant as ordering at a restaurant can be a distressful experience for them, making them the perfect audience for my platform.

On the Hunt for Competitors

The obvious method for learning how to order at any restaurant is by asking their employees directly. As my interview insights indicated, however, it’s not always an adequate solution—and it’s definitely not feasible for the majority of my target users.

You can always find suggestions online on what to order at build-your-own-meal restaurants, but it’s tricky to find useful information on how to order. A platform that focuses on providing those instructions doesn’t exist—although the closest alternative is wikiHow.

I analyzed relevant wikiHow guides to assess their usefulness, but I found that they’re vague, lengthy, and limited to only a few restaurants. Improving the content by rewriting them might solve these concerns, but there are still requirements from my users that wikiHow can’t implement.

Define

Defining the Problem and Objective

By empathizing with my users, I was able to determine the problem:

People with social anxiety go through an overwhelming experience when ordering in person at build-your-own-meal restaurants because they don’t have a comfortable way to learn how to order properly at such places.

One could argue that other approaches such as mobile ordering exist. Yes, people with social anxiety can still buy at build-your-own-meal restaurants without interacting with the employees—but at the end of the day, they still have that stress induced by the idea of talking to someone. I believe that by providing them with a better idea of what to do, they’ll be able to order comfortably right from the start. After all, social anxiety is manageable.

My objective was to create a learning platform on the seemingly complex workings of build-your-own-meal restaurants. I envisioned this solution as something like wikiHow or Wikipedia—though more interactive and not so text-heavy.

Everyone has browsed those platforms on the Web at one point instead of downloading the apps (Did you even know they had apps?). I expected my users would do the same, with them repeatedly referencing my platform until they feel well-informed enough to not return for a long time. Thus, it made sense to create a website instead of an app.

Crafting Personas

Using all the information my team and I acquired, I produced three personas of the platform’s potential users.

Ideate

From User Stories to User Flow

The platform has two primary functions: 1) inform users about the customizable menu items, and 2) teach them the ordering process at build-your-own-food restaurants. To figure out how it can accomplish these, my team and I listed all the requirements that will help users attain that comfortable ordering experience, then grouped the points that fall under a general pattern.

We distinguished the groups that will leave users with a general understanding of build-your-own-meal restaurants, and thereby, preventing the platform from being overcrowded with trivial features. I then transformed these fundamental points into user stories to get a clear picture of all the tasks related to the two functions.

Through the user stories, I established which tasks should be included in the platform. I mapped a user flow diagram to have a clear picture of the easiest way for users to reach their intended goals and tasks.

Sketches Galore

While referencing the user flow diagram, I began sketching ideas of the platform’s interfaces on paper.

Prototype

Wireframes and Prototypes

I built the wireframes using Sketch and the prototype on InVision.

The website’s critical component is the writing itself. I wrote real copy instead of putting dummy text since I wanted future testers to grasp the platform’s purpose by feeling like they’re actually learning something. My past experience as a barista trainer at Starbucks led to my using the coffeehouse chain for the sample guide.

Test

Usability Testing

While building the early versions of the website, I did some rapid prototyping with my collaborators and peers to see if the designs were going in the right direction regarding the project’s objective. When I felt more confident with the prototype, I conducted usability testing with four actual target users to evaluate its practicality and intuitiveness.

The testers’ unanimous feedback was that the journey to reach the Ordering Process pages was too long. Starting from the Home page, users had to visit a minimum of five screens to learn how to order at the restaurant. My mistake was putting a higher priority on the platform informing users about the restaurants’ menu items. The testers remarked that they actually care more about learning how to order than anything else.

As a result of all the feedback, I reiterated the designs so the prototype satisfies the target users’ needs better. The biggest change involved modifying the user flow diagram so users can learn how to order immediately after landing on the Restaurant Info page.

Results

Design Choices

Colour

For the main colour, I wanted something associated with food. I found yellow will make the interfaces overly bright, whereas red exudes the feeling of panic, which is the opposite of the platform’s intended outcome. I chose orange for it represents confidence and optimism, helping UpDish perfectly embody the role of an uplifting friend.

For the accent colour, although orange’s complementary colour of blue seems like a fitting choice since it represents renewal and relaxation, it’s seldomly associated with food. I chose green because it also represents growth and calmness, so my intended users will feel like they’re taking steps to overcome their anxiety in a nourishing environment. I settled on a cool hue of teal that contrasts with orange’s warmth.

Typography

For the primary typeface used for the headings, I chose FF Tisa Sans, of which the absence of serifs gives it that contemporary look. Best of all, it has this friendly-looking personality, achieved by its slightly rounded strokes and terminals.

For the secondary typeface used for the body text, I chose Pragmatica, a neutral typeface that harmonizes with my above choice. Both share the same x-height, while many of their glyphs are identical. Pragmatica’s rather stoical characteristic really brings out FF Tisa Sans’ overall friendly personality.

Reflection

Mobile-first Approach

Responsive web design provides the best user experience—but because this was a school project, I was tasked to focus on one device only. I decided to work on the desktop version since I didn’t want my teachers and classmates to misunderstand and think that I was building an app like everybody else. I only learned much later on that it’s better to follow a mobile-first approach so the design will translate better on all devices down the line.

Testing, Testing, and More Testing

After the initial usability testing, I worked with my collaborators to rearrange the content, enhance the copy, and improve the overall design and aesthetic of the interfaces. If I had more time, I would continue iterating and conducting more rounds of usability testing to see how well the optimizations are for my users.

Content First, Design Later!

As designers, I think we’re all guilty of wanting to jump straight into designing before having the content or research done first. When I was creating my personas—which was actually an assignment for one of my courses—I had all my notes from my interviews, but I spent a little too much time planning the design. I was too invested in making my submission pretty instead of synthesizing all my research first that I didn’t realize I was running out of time. I then spent all my energy putting together my personas and scenarios, but I ended up with a plain design so it won’t be late. I intended to redesign them, but I decided to keep them as a constant reminder of the important lesson I learned from that moment on: content first, design later!
